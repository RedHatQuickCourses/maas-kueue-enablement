GPU-as-a-Service Architecture

Estimated reading time: 30 minutes.

The Multi-Tenant GPU Scarcity Problem

Organizations running AI/ML workloads on OpenShift face a shared GPU scarcity problem: multiple teams—such as inference serving and model training—compete for the same limited pool of GPUs. Without intelligent quota management, this creates operational chaos.

Consider a typical scenario: Your organization has a 4-GPU cluster. The inference team deploys a customer-facing model serving API that needs guaranteed low latency to meet SLA commitments. The training team runs long-running model fine-tuning jobs. Both teams submit workloads to Kubernetes with ResourceQuota limits, but Kubernetes scheduling is rigid—it either admits a workload immediately or rejects it. There’s no queueing, no priority-based preemption, no way to say "inference workloads are more critical than training."

What happens in practice:

  • Monday 9 AM: Training team submits 4 large training jobs, consuming all 4 GPUs

  • Monday 10 AM: Inference team tries to scale their serving deployment to handle production traffic

  • Result: Inference workloads are rejected. Kubernetes scheduler returns "insufficient nvidia.com/gpu resources." Customer-facing API latency spikes. Platform team receives an urgent Slack ping.

  • Manual intervention: Platform engineer spends 2 hours manually identifying training jobs, contacting the training team, negotiating which jobs to kill, and restarting inference workloads.

This manual firefighting happens multiple times per week.

Business Impact Without GPU-as-a-Service

Problem Business Impact

Resource starvation

Critical inference workloads wait while training jobs consume all GPUs → Customer-facing APIs degrade, SLA violations, revenue impact

Unpredictable scheduling

No guarantees about when workloads will start → Teams over-request GPUs "just in case," leading to idle resources and planning paralysis

Manual intervention

Platform team manually adjusts priorities and terminates jobs → 20+ hours/week firefighting, high operational toil, team burnout

Poor GPU utilization

Rigid ResourceQuotas prevent elastic sharing → GPU utilization drops to 40%, wasting $150K/year on idle hardware (assuming 4x A100 GPUs at $3/hour each)

Solution: GPU-as-a-Service with Kueue

This chapter implements a GPU-as-a-Service model that provides:

  1. Guaranteed GPU quotas per team with elastic borrowing when capacity is idle

  2. Priority-based preemption so high-priority inference workloads can automatically reclaim GPUs from training

  3. Self-service integration via OpenShift AI dashboard Hardware Profiles

  4. Real-time monitoring with Grafana dashboards tracking utilization, borrowing, and preemption

Result: GPU utilization increases from 40% to 85-90%, inference SLAs are protected with automatic preemption within 30 seconds, training throughput becomes predictable, and manual intervention drops to near zero.

GPU-as-a-Service Architecture

The GPU-as-a-Service reference architecture uses a single Cohort (introduced in Chapter 2) to manage a shared pool of 4 GPUs across two teams: inference and training. The cohort enables elastic quota sharing—teams can borrow idle quota from each other—while asymmetric preemption policies (introduced in Chapter 3) ensure inference workloads can always reclaim GPUs when needed.

GPU Quota Allocation

Key design decisions:

  • Inference gets 3 guaranteed GPUs: Even if training consumes the entire cluster, inference is guaranteed 3 GPUs. This protects customer-facing SLAs.

  • Inference can borrow 1 additional GPU: When training is idle, inference can use all 4 GPUs (3 guaranteed + 1 borrowed).

  • Training gets 1 guaranteed GPU: Training is guaranteed at least 1 GPU, preventing complete starvation.

  • Training can borrow 2 additional GPUs: When inference is idle, training can use up to 3 GPUs (1 guaranteed + 2 borrowed). Training is limited to 3 max to ensure at least 1 GPU is always available for inference to reclaim if needed.

Asymmetric Preemption Rules

The architecture implements asymmetric preemption to protect high-priority inference workloads. This is the key innovation that transforms a simple quota system into a production-grade GPU-as-a-Service platform.

Asymmetric preemption means: Inference can preempt training, but training cannot preempt inference.

This is achieved through carefully configured preemption policies in the ClusterQueue resources (introduced in Chapter 3).

Inference ClusterQueue Preemption Policies

The inference-cq ClusterQueue has aggressive preemption policies:

apiVersion: kueue.x-k8s.io/v1beta2
kind: ClusterQueue
metadata:
  name: inference-cq
spec:
  preemption:
    reclaimWithinCohort: Any                    (1)
    borrowWithinCohort:
      policy: LowerPriority                     (2)
      maxPriorityThreshold: 100                 (3)
    withinClusterQueue: LowerPriority           (4)
  cohortName: gpu-cohort
  # ... resourceGroups omitted for clarity
1 reclaimWithinCohort: Any: Allows inference to reclaim its lent quota from any workload in the cohort, regardless of priority. If training borrowed inference’s idle GPUs and inference needs them back, inference preempts training immediately.
2 borrowWithinCohort.policy: LowerPriority: Allows inference to preempt lower-priority workloads in the cohort when borrowing beyond its guaranteed quota.
3 maxPriorityThreshold: 100: Only preempts workloads with priority ≤ 100. Since training has priority 100 and inference has priority 1000, inference can preempt training but not other high-priority inference workloads.
4 withinClusterQueue: LowerPriority: Within the inference queue itself, lower-priority workloads are preempted first.

Practical effects:

  • ✅ Inference can preempt training to borrow GPUs: If inference needs a 4th GPU and training is using GPUs, inference can preempt a training workload to borrow from the cohort.

  • ✅ Inference can always reclaim its lent quota: If training borrowed inference’s idle GPUs (e.g., inference was using 2 GPUs, training borrowed the idle 1 GPU), and inference now needs its full 3 guaranteed GPUs, inference preempts training regardless of priority.

  • ✅ Inference protects critical workloads: Within the inference queue, lower-priority jobs are preempted first, protecting high-priority serving workloads.

Training ClusterQueue Preemption Policies

The training-cq ClusterQueue has limited preemption policies:

apiVersion: kueue.x-k8s.io/v1beta2
kind: ClusterQueue
metadata:
  name: training-cq
spec:
  preemption:
    reclaimWithinCohort: LowerPriority          (1)
    # NO borrowWithinCohort policy              (2)
    withinClusterQueue: LowerPriority           (3)
  cohortName: gpu-cohort
  # ... resourceGroups omitted for clarity
1 reclaimWithinCohort: LowerPriority: Training can only reclaim its lent quota by preempting workloads with priority < 100. Since inference has priority 1000, training cannot reclaim from inference.
2 No borrowWithinCohort policy: Training has no borrowWithinCohort policy, meaning training cannot preempt other workloads to borrow. Training can only use GPUs that are genuinely idle in the cohort.
3 withinClusterQueue: LowerPriority: Lower-priority training jobs are preempted first within the training queue.

Practical effects:

  • ❌ Training cannot preempt inference to borrow GPUs: Training can only borrow from the cohort if GPUs are idle. It cannot force inference workloads off GPUs.

  • ⚠️ Training reclaim is limited: Training can only reclaim its lent quota from workloads with priority < 100. Since inference has priority 1000, training cannot reclaim from inference.

  • ✅ Training protects within its own queue: Lower-priority training jobs are preempted first.

Never configure training with a borrowWithinCohort policy in production. This would allow training to preempt inference, breaking inference SLA guarantees. Asymmetric preemption is the critical design pattern that protects customer-facing workloads.

Hardware Profiles for Self-Service

Hardware Profiles (introduced in OpenShift AI 3.3+) provide self-service GPU selection in the OpenShift AI dashboard. Data scientists select a profile when launching a workbench or serving runtime, and the platform automatically routes the workload to the correct LocalQueue with the appropriate priority.

This eliminates the need for data scientists to understand Kueue internals—they simply choose "highPriority" or "lowPriority" from a dropdown.

User Experience Workflow

When a data scientist uses a Hardware Profile in the OpenShift AI dashboard, the following workflow executes automatically:

  1. Data scientist opens OpenShift AI dashboard

    • Navigates to Data Science Projects → Select project → Workbenches

  2. Clicks "Create Workbench"

    • Provides workbench name, notebook image, and resource requests

  3. Selects hardware profile: "highPriority"

    • Chooses from dropdown: highPriority or lowPriority

    • Adjusts GPU count slider: 1-4 GPUs for highPriority

  4. Clicks "Create"

    • Workbench creation request submitted

  5. Platform automatically routes workload (behind the scenes):

    • OpenShift AI injects label: kueue.x-k8s.io/queue-name: team-inference

    • OpenShift AI injects label: kueue.x-k8s.io/priority-class: inference-priority

    • Kueue detects labels and routes workload to team-inference LocalQueue

    • LocalQueue routes to inference-cq ClusterQueue

    • Kueue admits workload based on quota and preemption policies

    • Kubernetes scheduler places workload on GPU node (via ResourceFlavor node selectors)

  6. Data scientist sees workload status in dashboard

    • Pending: Waiting for quota (queued in Kueue)

    • Running: Admitted and scheduled

Hardware Profiles provide a zero-touch Kueue integration for data scientists. They don’t need to know about ClusterQueues, LocalQueues, or WorkloadPriorityClasses. The platform team configures these mappings once, and data scientists get automatic queue routing and priority assignment based on their hardware selection.

Hardware Profile Configuration Example

The highpriority Hardware Profile YAML shows the key configuration fields:

apiVersion: infrastructure.opendatahub.io/v1
kind: HardwareProfile
metadata:
  name: highpriority
  namespace: redhat-ods-applications              (1)
  annotations:
    opendatahub.io/display-name: highPriority
spec:
  identifiers:
  - identifier: nvidia.com/gpu                    (2)
    displayName: NVIDIA L4
    resourceType: Accelerator
    minCount: 1
    maxCount: 4                                   (3)
    defaultCount: 1
  scheduling:
    type: Queue                                   (4)
    kueue:
      localQueueName: team-inference              (5)
      priorityClass: inference-priority           (6)
1 namespace: redhat-ods-applications: Hardware Profiles must be created in the OpenShift AI operator namespace.
2 identifier: nvidia.com/gpu: Specifies GPU resource type (matches Kubernetes resource name).
3 maxCount: 4: Matches the inference ClusterQueue’s max usable GPUs (3 guaranteed + 1 borrowed).
4 scheduling.type: Queue: Enables Kueue integration (alternative: type: None for direct scheduling).
5 localQueueName: team-inference: Routes workloads to the team-inference LocalQueue in the team-inference namespace.
6 priorityClass: inference-priority: Assigns priority 1000, enabling preemption of training workloads.

The lowpriority Hardware Profile has similar structure but routes to team-training LocalQueue with training-priority (value: 100) and maxCount: 3.

Production Considerations

When to Use Separate Cohorts vs. Single Cohort

The reference architecture uses a single cohort for simplicity and maximum GPU utilization. However, some scenarios require separate cohorts for strict isolation.

Use Case Recommendation Reason

Inference and training teams, both using GPUs regularly

Single cohort

Maximize utilization via borrowing. Inference can reclaim when needed via preemption.

Separate business units with strict isolation requirements

Separate cohorts

No quota borrowing across BUs. Each BU has guaranteed capacity with no cross-BU preemption.

Mix of preemptible (training) and non-preemptible (inference) workloads

Single cohort with preemption policies

Protect inference via asymmetric preemption (this chapter’s pattern).

Development and production environments

Separate cohorts

Prevent dev workloads from affecting prod. Use separate ClusterQueues in different cohorts.

Multi-region deployments with geo-specific quotas

Separate cohorts per region

Each region’s cohort maps to that region’s GPU node pool. No cross-region borrowing.

For revenue-generating MaaS platforms, start with a single cohort and asymmetric preemption. Only split into multiple cohorts if you have strict isolation requirements (compliance, multi-tenancy SLAs, geographic boundaries). Premature cohort splitting reduces GPU utilization by preventing elastic quota sharing.

Sizing Recommendations

Cluster Size Recommended Cohort Strategy Notes

< 8 GPUs

Single cohort, 2-3 ClusterQueues

Simple, effective for small teams. Use asymmetric preemption to protect critical workloads.

8-32 GPUs

2-3 cohorts (by workload type: inference, training, interactive)

Balance fairness and utilization. Example: inference-cohort (12 GPUs), training-cohort (16 GPUs), dev-cohort (4 GPUs).

> 32 GPUs

Multiple cohorts + hierarchical quotas

Consider geographic or org-unit based cohorts. Use namespaceSelector to isolate tenants.

Monitoring and Alerting Preview

Effective GPU-as-a-Service operations require real-time visibility. Section 4 (Monitoring and Observability) covers the Grafana dashboard in detail, but key metrics to watch include:

  • Quota utilization: kueue_cluster_queue_resource_usage / kueue_cluster_queue_nominal_quota > 0.95 indicates quota exhaustion

  • Pending workloads: kueue_pending_workloads > 10 for 5+ minutes indicates backlog

  • Eviction rate: rate(kueue_evicted_workloads_total[1h]) > 5 indicates high preemption activity
